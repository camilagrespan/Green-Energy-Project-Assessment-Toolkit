How to Use This Toolkit

Purpose & Navigation
Make decisions that are truly informed, truly free, and truly yours.

This toolkit is designed for Tribal Governments, community members, and allied organizations navigating energy project proposals on or near sovereign lands. As supporters of the Free, Prior, and Informed Consent principles, our aim is to respectfully support Tribal Sovereignty from the perspective of justice. This toolkit is not intended to tell communities what to decide, but rather provides transparent information and guiding questions to make decisions that are truly informed, truly free, and truly yours.

Navigating emerging technologies with complex definitions, regulations, and high greenwashing potential.

Energy developers approaching Native lands are increasingly pitching hydrogen and waste-to-energy projects as "clean," "green," or "zero-emission" solutions. While there are many climate solution technologies being marketed today, in this toolkit we chose to focus on these technologies because they are often emerging technologies with complex definitions and representations, complicated regulations, and as a result face high greenwashing potential. Developer claims can be misleading, and the technical complexity of the projects makes them difficult to evaluate without specialized knowledge. This toolkit translates that technical complexity into accessible, actionable information.

How to Navigate

If a developer has already approached your community

Start with Section 06 (The Greenwashing Guide) to identify potential red flag language in their pitch, then check Section 05 (The Companies) to read case studies on companies that have been involved in these projects. Use Section 07 (The Due Diligence Checklist) before any formal engagement or as soon as possible if engagement has already begun.

If you want to understand the technologies

Start with Sections 02 and 03 for plain-language overviews of hydrogen and waste-to-energy technologies, including what each one actually emits and what the potential environmental and health risks are associated with each technology.

If you want to understand your legal and regulatory rights

Go to Section 04 (The Regulations) for an overview of the agencies involved, how regulatory gaps are exploited, and how to ensure Free, Prior, and Informed Consent is respected.

If you want to explore alternatives

See Section 08 (The Alternatives) for an overview of alternative renewable energy options and how to achieve a Just Transition while maintaining Tribal energy sovereignty.

A note on framing: This toolkit uses the term "false solutions" to describe projects that are marketed as clean or green but fail to deliver genuine environmental or community benefit. This framing is commonly used in climate justice and environmental justice circles. However, the toolkit recognizes that not all "green" energy technologies are equivalent, and that technologies which are produced from truly renewable energy sources have legitimate climate potential and as such are treated differently throughout this document.

The Technologies: Hydrogen

Production Methods, Environmental & Health Risks

Hydrogen energy is not a single technology—rather, it can be produced using a spectrum of methods with very different environmental profiles. The color-coding system below (green, blue, grey) is commonly used in the industry, but developers are not always clear about which type of hydrogen technology they are utilizing, and in some cases may employ one technology as a "bridge" to transition to another. Additionally, there are other production methods such as turquoise, pink, white, black, and brown, which while not as common today are still worth mentioning in the event a developer proposes one of these technologies. (Hydrogen | MIT Climate Portal) Below, we provide a brief overview of the production methods, environmental, and health risks associated with the most common types of hydrogen technologies (blue, green, and grey), as well as short summaries describing the other less common production methods (turquoise, pink, white, brown and black).

In some cases, developers and regulatory agencies may consider one technology as 'clean' by means of other technologies, such as Carbon Capture & Storage (CCS), that offset or repurpose carbon emissions. It is important to always ask informed guiding questions that help communities understand which technology is actually being developed, what infrastructure is being built, where energy and water resources are coming from, and what the environmental and health impacts and risks are.

TECHNOLOGY PRODUCTION METHOD ENVIRONMENTAL RISKS HEALTH RISKS IMPORTANT NOTES
GREY Uses high-heat steam to split natural gas (methane) into hydrogen and carbon dioxide, also known as Steam Methane Reforming (SMR), releasing greenhouse gas pollution into the atmosphere in the process.
  • High carbon dioxide emissions.
  • Natural gas extraction releases methane, a greenhouse gas far more powerful than CO2.
  • Uses significant water resources.
  • Air pollution released during production can worsen air quality and contribute to respiratory issues.
  • Communities near natural gas wells face high potential for contaminated groundwater and air from fracking.
 Currently the most common form of hydrogen production globally.
BLUE Also uses SMR, but adds a step to "capture" the emissions by combining carbon capture and storage (CCS) technology to pump carbon dioxide underground for storage, and in some cases distribution for secondary use.
  • Relies on natural gas, which maintains extractive patterns like drilling and fracking.
  • Carbon capture technology today is inefficient and unproven at scale, and can fail to capture other forms of pollution.
  • Capturing carbons requires extra cooling, which uses even more water than grey hydrogen.
  • Air quality impacts from uncaptured pollution such as nitrous oxide and particulate matter can contribute to respiratory issues.
  • Repurposing gas pipelines for hydrogen transport carries significant safety risks including potential leakage and explosions.
 While marketed as a "clean" alternative, it still requires fossil fuel extraction for production.
GREEN Electrolysis of water (splitting water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen) using renewable electricity (solar or wind) that is additive (not by using existing renewable energy sources). No fossil fuels involved in production.
  • Requires high energy and water consumption.
  • Large land footprint for associated renewable infrastructure.
  • Risks from hydrogen leakage can contribute to global warming.
  • Minimal direct health risks if produced cleanly, however safety risks from explosion and leakage in pipelines, storage, and transport facilities still exist if not planned for and managed properly.
 In order to be considered truly green, production must meet the three pillars of clean hydrogen production: it must use clean energy sources which are new (incrementality), now (temporality), and near (deliverability).

Other Hydrogen Production Technologies

TURQUOISE This technology uses pyrolysis to heat natural gas to produce hydrogen and solid carbon instead of CO2 gas, eliminating the need for carbon capture and creating a by-product in the form of elemental carbon.
PINK Like green hydrogen, pink hydrogen technology also uses electrolysis for production but is powered by nuclear power rather than wind or solar. It is considered carbon-free due to the use of nuclear energy, however carries the long-term safety and waste risks associated with the nuclear industry.
WHITE While rare in today's technology landscape, this utilizes hydrogen gas that is found naturally in underground deposits. A fairly new discovery, this emerging technology relies on hydrogen extraction through industrial drilling, which can disrupt local ecosystems.
BLACK &
BROWN		 Similar to grey hydrogen, these technologies also rely on fossil fuels and are produced from coal through a high-heat process called gasification. These are the most environmentally damaging forms of hydrogen due to massive CO2 emissions and reliance on destructive coal mining.

Hydrogen Hubs & Pipelines

In recent years, there has been growing momentum for the development of regional hydrogen hubs, which are large-scale industrial centers where hydrogen is produced, stored, and transported. To connect these hubs to distant markets, developers are proposing massive pipeline networks such as the Western Interstate Hydrogen Hub (WISHH) in the central Rocky Mountains, and the Alliance for Renewable Clean Hydrogen Energy Systems (ARCHES) in California, and sometimes cross Tribal lands. These projects frequently involve building new pipelines or repurposing old ones to carry hydrogen or capture CO2, which can create serious safety risks for local communities, including the potential for high-pressure leaks and the further industrialization of sovereign territory.

Furthermore, a recent scientific review highlights that because hydrogen molecules are much smaller and lighter than natural gas, they leak far more easily through seals, joints, and even intact pipeline materials, significantly increasing the risk of accidents and equipment failure (Martin et al., 2024)(A review of challenges with using the natural gas system for hydrogen - Martin - 2024 - Energy Science & Engineering - Wiley Online Library).

Key Takeaways for Communities

  • Always ask which type of hydrogen is being proposed and what the energy source is. A project simply called "hydrogen" or "clean hydrogen" is not automatically green hydrogen. If possible, seek external third party verification to assess technologies being proposed.
  • Blue hydrogen is dependent on natural gas, which is a fossil fuel. While blue hydrogen is supposed to utilize carbon capture technologies to offset emissions, it is important to question exactly how those emissions will be offset. For example, if carbon emissions are to be "stored" underground (such as in Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)), where are they being stored and what are the environmental impacts of the storage technology? If the carbon captured is to be repurposed and sold (such as in carbon sales to soda beverage companies), what are the environmental impacts of transporting the carbon byproduct to those companies?
  • Green hydrogen has legitimate climate potential but carries significant water use implications—a critical concern for communities with existing water rights conflicts. It is critical to understand where the water used to produce green hydrogen is coming from, and whether this impacts communities already burdened by access to clean and dependable water sources.
  • Many projects claiming federal "clean hydrogen" incentives under the IRA are blue, not green. Ask for detailed documentation of the project's energy source and planned infrastructure before any discussions proceed.

Important note: during the Biden-era administration and their introduction of the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), Section 45V of the IRA specifically designated incentives for the development of hydrogen energy projects, which sparked large-scale financing of hydrogen hubs. After the 2025 Trump administration took office and implemented the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), the timelines for projects depending on those incentives was cut short, resulting in withdrawals of funding for several large-scale projects. For those projects in which financial backing remained, the drastically reduced timeline to claim incentives under the IRA may have resulted in projects being pushed forward under circumstances which were not initially planned for.

The Technologies: Waste-to-Energy

Incineration, Gasification & Pyrolysis

This section explores Waste-to-Energy (WTE) technologies, which are often marketed to communities as a "win-win" solution for managing local waste while generating "green" electricity. However, it is critical to distinguish between different types of waste processing. While Biological methods (like composting or anaerobic digestion) use natural processes to break down organic waste, this toolkit focuses on Thermal WTE—technologies that use extreme heat to process municipal trash, plastics, and industrial waste.

The purpose of this section is to demystify the technical jargon used by developers to rebrand high-heat processes. Many modern WTE proposals are essentially incinerators in disguise, utilizing combustion, gasification, or pyrolysis to burn or chemically alter waste. By understanding the underlying mechanics of these thermal systems, communities can better evaluate how these projects impact local air quality, water safety, and long-term climate goals.

TECHNOLOGY PRODUCTION METHOD ENVIRONMENTAL RISKS HEALTH RISKS
INCINERATION Direct Combustion: Unsorted municipal waste is fed into a furnace and burned at high temperatures to produce steam for electricity. High Carbon & Toxins: Releases massive amounts of CO2 and heavy metals directly into the atmosphere through the stack. Air Quality: Constant release of fine particulate matter (PM2.5) linked to asthma, heart disease, and lung cancer.
GASIFICATION Partially oxidizes waste at high heat to produce syngas (a mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen), which is then combusted for energy. Produces tar, slag, and contaminated wastewater. Syngas combustion releases CO2, NOx, and particulates. Ash contains concentrated heavy metals. Carcinogen emissions including benzene and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). Worker exposure risk. Community air quality impacts depend heavily on facility management quality.
PYROLYSIS Heats waste in the absence of oxygen to produce bio-oil, syngas, and char. Sometimes marketed as "chemical recycling" or "advanced recycling." Produces hazardous liquid and solid byproducts. Emission profiles are difficult to characterize fully. Energy-intensive process may require fossil fuel inputs. Poorly regulated. Limited long-term health data. PFAS ("forever chemicals") and heavy metals can concentrate in char, which may be marketed as fertilizer — posing serious soil and groundwater contamination risks.
PLASMA ARC Uses a high-temperature electric arc (up to 5,000°C) to thermally decompose waste into syngas and solid slag. Often combined with gasification and marketed as "non-combustion" technology. Produces syngas that is subsequently combusted, releasing CO2, NOx, and other pollutants. Slag disposal poses environmental risks. Extremely energy-intensive, often requires fossil fuel power input exceeding energy output. High operational and safety risks. Poorly understood long-term health impacts. History of facility failures and project abandonment — most notably Plasco Energy Group's $300M plasma arc facility in Ottawa that collapsed in 2018.

Waste-to-Hydrogen

Waste-to-Hydrogen (WTH) is an emerging thermal technology that combines gasification or pyrolysis with hydrogen production, often marketed as a "clean" way to eliminate local waste while fueling the future. While some developers claim to use only "organic" waste or biomass, these facilities are often designed to process a wide variety of feedstocks, including municipal solid waste, industrial plastics, and sewage sludge. From an environmental justice perspective, WTH is simply a more complex version of an incinerator in disguise. The process typically involves heating these materials to create a "syngas," which is then chemically treated to extract hydrogen molecules. Even when feedstocks are labeled as "organic," there is a significant potential for contamination from heavy metals, microplastics, and chemical residues often found in waste streams. Consequently, these processes risk releasing hazardous air emissions and producing toxic residue while requiring significant energy and water inputs to function.

Furthermore, many developers are now partnering with Regional Hydrogen Hubs to access massive amounts of federal Department of Energy (DOE) funding and rebrand their facilities as "clean hydrogen" companies. This "identity cloaking" makes these projects much harder for communities to identify and track; a facility branded as a "hydrogen hub" may, in fact, be a thermal waste gasification plant that carries the inherent risks of a traditional incinerator. Regardless of whether a feedstock is classified as organic waste or plastic, this rebranding serves to prop up high-heat "false solutions" that create a financial incentive to keep importing waste into a community, potentially undermining Tribal sovereign efforts toward Zero Waste and long-term environmental stewardship.

The "No-Burn" Claim

Several gasification and pyrolysis companies market their technologies as "non-combustion" or "no-burn." This is a technicality and can be misleading. While the initial waste processing stage may not involve direct combustion, the syngas or bio-oil produced is subsequently burned to generate power, releasing the same pollutants the "gasification" stage supposedly avoided.

The "Zero Waste" Claim

No thermal WTE process produces zero waste. All technologies generate solid residues—such as ash, slag, or char—that carry a significant potential for contamination from heavy metals and persistent chemicals. "Zero waste to landfill" claims are often an accounting trick where toxic residues are reclassified and shipped to other locations, frequently targeting communities with fewer regulatory protections.

The Regulations

How Projects Get Approved — and How Protections Break Down

Environmental regulation on Tribal lands is defined by a fragmented jurisdictional framework where federal, state, and Tribal authorities often overlap. Under federal law, state governments generally lack the authority to enforce environmental regulations on Tribal trust lands unless specifically authorized by Congress (Kronk Warner, 2021). However, federal agencies often fail to establish specific environmental standards for Tribal Nations, creating a "regulatory gap" (Kronk Warner, 2021). While Tribes possess inherent sovereign authority to regulate their environment, they must navigate federal oversight mechanisms, primarily the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) in which the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) holds sole responsibility and discretion, whenever a project involves federal funding or land approvals (BIA NEPA Handbook; Timalsina et al., 2025).

A critical limitation of Tribal jurisdiction involves non-member activity on "fee land" (land owned by non-Indians within a reservation). Under the Supreme Court's ruling in Montana v. United States (1981), Tribal regulatory authority over non-members on fee land is restricted unless the activity threatens the political integrity, economic security, or health and welfare of the Tribe. Developers often exploit this legal precedent by siting the most high-risk components of a project on fee land to argue that Tribal environmental codes do not apply (Earthjustice Notes; Montana v. U.S., 1981).

Consultation vs. Consent (FPIC)

Under federal law, agencies are required to engage in "meaningful consultation" with Tribes (Executive Order 13175; NHPA Section 106). However, in practice, consultation is a procedural hurdle rather than a requirement for Tribal agreement. This means federal agencies can effectively "check a box" by holding a meeting and then approve a project over a community's explicit objections if the developer meets minimum technical criteria (BIA Handbook; Timalsina et al., 2025). Furthermore, developers often exploit this lack of legal teeth by conducting "consultation" only after project designs are finalized, engaging only with elected leadership rather than the broader community, and framing one-way presentations as if the community has given its permission (Earthjustice Notes).

To ensure genuine protection, communities must advocate for Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC). Unlike standard consultation, FPIC is an internationally recognized right that requires consent to be given without coercion, obtained before any project activities begin, and based on complete, accessible information. Because FPIC is not yet legally binding in the U.S., it is often treated as an optional PR exercise by corporations rather than a mandatory safeguard. True sovereignty requires moving beyond receiving a presentation to having the recognized power to say "No." Unless a Tribe codifies its own independent environmental and safety codes that mandate FPIC, it remains vulnerable to federal agencies that may prioritize national energy goals over local Tribal safety (BIA Handbook; Timalsina et al., 2025).

Agency Roles and Authority

Agency Role Operational Reality & Gaps
Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) Enforces the Clean Air Act and Clean Water Act. Under CWA Section 518, Tribes can apply for Treatment as a State (TAS) (EPA, 2017). EPA regulates underground carbon injection (Class VI wells) and sets incinerator emission limits. However, it currently lacks the mandate to address above-ground carbon leakage or the specific toxicity of "biochar" or ash produced by WTE gasification (Earthjustice Notes).
Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) Approves surface leases and Rights-of-Way (ROW) under 25 CFR Part 169. Manages the READI initiative for energy deployment (BIA, 2023; Timalsina et al., 2025). BIA has a fiduciary "Trust Responsibility" to protect Tribal lands, but is chronically understaffed, often leading to perfunctory environmental reviews and delayed safety oversight (Timalsina et al., 2025; BIA Handbook).
Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA) Oversees pipeline safety under 49 CFR Part 192 (Timalsina et al., 2025). Existing federal safety standards were designed for natural gas. They do not yet fully account for hydrogen's unique risks, such as embrittlement (metal cracking) or the lack of compatible leak-detection odorants (Timalsina et al., 2025; CATF, 2024).
Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) Regulates interstate energy markets and natural gas pipelines under the Natural Gas Act (NGA). Major Gap: Because hydrogen is not legally defined as "natural gas," FERC currently lacks the authority to "site" (dictate the location of) hydrogen-only pipelines. This leaves a "siting vacuum" where no single federal agency ensures safe pipeline placement (CATF, 2024).
Department of Energy (DOE) Administers IRA funding and the National Clean Hydrogen Strategy (DOE, 2023). Primarily a funding body. Its approval signifies a project meets national energy goals, but it does not constitute an environmental or safety permit. DOE "Community Benefit Plans" are administrative checkboxes and are rarely legally enforceable by the Tribe (Earthjustice Notes; DOE Roadmap).

Common Developer Bypass Strategies

Project Segmentation ("Piecemealing")

Developers frequently define a project narrowly—for example, focusing only on the "carbon capture facility"—while excluding the associated pipelines and source facilities from the initial environmental review (Earthjustice Notes). This avoids a comprehensive assessment of the "cumulative impact" of the entire industrial infrastructure (Timalsina et al., 2025).

Categorical Exclusions (CATEX)

Developers lobby agencies for a CATEX designation, which exempts a project from detailed NEPA analysis (BIA NEPA Handbook). This is commonly used for "pilot programs" or components framed as routine infrastructure, despite the potential for long-term industrial impact (Earthjustice Notes).

The "Novelty" Shield

Companies often claim their technology is "novel" to argue that existing regulations do not apply or to discourage community opposition by claiming the risks are too complex for non-experts to evaluate (Earthjustice Notes). In reality, technologies like Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) have decades of inconsistent performance data (Earthjustice Notes).

Funding-Based Urgency

Developers use the expiration of Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) tax credits (such as the 45V credit) or DOE grant cycles to pressure Tribal Councils into signing agreements (DOE Roadmap). This "urgency" is a tactic used to bypass the time-consuming process of meaningful community consultation and independent technical review (Earthjustice Notes).

Jurisdictional Shell Games

Developers may move project components between trust land and fee land to exploit areas where neither state nor federal agencies have clear enforcement authority, effectively operating in a regulatory vacuum (Kronk Warner, 2021; Earthjustice Notes).

05

The Companies

Case Studies of Hydrogen and WTE Companies

The following section presents case studies of companies which are involved in hydrogen or WTE project development in recent years. Each profile uses publicly available information, compiled through research and analysis of various resources including concept papers, organizational reports, news articles, and interview notes. These studies are not exhaustive, and are only meant to provide a cursory glance of how we perceive these companies have conducted community engagement—communities should conduct their own research and seek representation prior to making any decisions.

Tallgrass Energy / GreenView Logistics
Hydrogen Pipeline & Infrastructure  ·  Western Hydrogen Hub
The Technology
Tallgrass is a traditional oil and gas pipeline company transitioning into the "clean energy" space by repurposing existing fossil fuel infrastructure. This company plans to utilize "Green and/or Blue Hydrogen" production and Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS). Additionally, they have plans for pipeline conversion, which involves modifying old oil or natural gas pipelines to transport highly pressurized hydrogen gas.
Project Overview
Large-scale hydrogen pipeline and distribution infrastructure as part of the Western Interstate Hydrogen Hub (WISHH). Pitches economic development and energy transition partnership to Tribal governments along proposed pipeline routes. The most notable projects that Tallgrass has recently been involved in is the conversion of the retired Escalante Power Plant in Prewitt, New Mexico, into a hydrogen production facility. This project is a key part of the Western Interstates Hydrogen Hub (WISHH), a multi-state coalition which was not awarded federal funding from the DOE, though reports indicate that partners are still committed to development. To transport the hydrogen and captured CO2, Tallgrass proposed a massive pipeline network—including the "Trailblazer" pipeline—to connect production sites across the Southwest to industrial markets.
Community Engagement
Proposed hydrogen pipeline would cross or border multiple Tribal nations in the Southwest. The Navajo Nation has been specifically targeted — community members have raised alarms about potential impacts on water supply. Reports indicate that many residents, particularly within the Navajo Nation, felt "blindsided" by the company's plans. While Tallgrass participated in industry-led roundtables and high-level meetings with Tribal Governments, community members have reported a lack of transparent communication regarding how pipeline routes would change or how a hydrogen leak would be managed on their lands. Critics argue that "engagement" has been top-down, focusing on securing political support rather than obtaining the Free, Prior, and Informed Consent of the people living near the infrastructure.
Key Takeaways
  • Gas Expansion: Despite being marketed as "green," this project requires continued fracking and natural gas extraction to provide the feedstock for hydrogen, locking the region into fossil fuel dependency
  • Safety & Water: Hydrogen is highly leak-prone and can make older metal pipelines brittle and dangerous. Community advocates also worry about the massive amount of water required for hydrogen production in an already drought-stricken region
  • Environmental Justice: By repurposing and changing infrastructure plans without deep community consent, they risk repeating the patterns of the fossil fuel era
BayoTech
Hydrogen  ·  Stockton, California  ·  ARCHES Hub Partner
The Technology
BayoTech primarily uses Steam Methane Reforming (SMR) to produce grey hydrogen from natural gas (methane), and some blue hydrogen with the addition of CCS technology. The company uses several labels for their hydrogen products, though there are misleading claims on what hydrogen technology is being utilized. For example, they claim that when their process is paired with Carbon Capture or Renewable Natural Gas (RNG), it becomes "fully renewable green hydrogen" (https://bayotech.us/overview/). This is technically incorrect: pairing methane with carbon capture creates Blue Hydrogen, not Green. True Green Hydrogen is produced only through water electrolysis powered by wind or solar energy.
Project Overview
BayoTech is developing "local hydrogen production hubs," (https://bayotech.us/overview/) such as the facility at the Port of Stockton. These projects are designed to produce thousands of kilograms of hydrogen daily for heavy-duty trucking. Even when marketed as "green" or "renewable," these facilities require new natural gas pipeline hookups and significant local water consumption, often situated in already overburdened industrial areas.
Community Engagement
The Center for Biological Diversity, Earthjustice, and the Sierra Club filed a lawsuit against the Port of Stockton for approving this "dirty hydrogen" project. Advocates argue the Port used a "Mitigated Negative Declaration" to bypass a full Environmental Impact Report (EIR), effectively bypassing community engagement in a region that already suffers from some of the worst air quality in California. The lawsuit claims the Port failed to account for increased air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions from the new gas pipeline and production facility.
Key Takeaways
  • Greenwashing: Using the term "Green" and "low-carbon" for gas-based projects to secure clean energy funding and public favor
  • Regulatory Bypass: Using modularity and "minor" impact claims to avoid the rigorous environmental scrutiny that a full industrial plant would normally require
H Cycle
WTE and Waste-to-Hydrogen  ·  Pittsburg, California  ·  ARCHES Hub Partner
The Technology
H Cycle uses a high-heat thermal conversion process to break down municipal solid waste—including organic waste and mixed plastics—to produce hydrogen gas. While the company markets its process as "non-combustion," environmental groups point to the venting of CO2 and the production of "slag" (a glass-like solid waste) as evidence that the facility carries risks similar to traditional incineration.
Project Overview
A major H Cycle initiative is the Pittsburg Renewable Hydrogen Project in California. The facility is designed to process roughly 385 tons of waste per day to produce 25 tons of hydrogen. H Cycle is a partner in ARCHES, the California hydrogen hub awarded DOE federal funding under the Biden era IRA. The company positions the project as a solution for state waste mandates, promising to divert "organic" material from landfills.
Community Engagement
The project has faced formal opposition from a coalition of environmental justice and health organizations, including Greenaction, the Sierra Club, and the Natural Resources Defense Council (NRDC). These groups filed a detailed challenge to the project's Environmental Impact Report (EIR), alleging that the company failed to provide data on toxic air emissions and the exact chemical makeup of the "slag" byproduct. Critics argue that the facility will add a new pollution burden to an already heavily industrialized community.
Key Takeaways
  • The "Zero Waste" Conflict: These plants require a massive, guaranteed supply of waste to operate profitably, which can discourage local efforts to prioritize true recycling and composting.
  • Carbon-Negative Claims: Advocacy groups warn that the company's "carbon-negative" claims are based on flawed accounting that ignores the emissions created during the gasification process and the transportation of waste.
  • Toxic Residue: The process generates slag that may be classified as hazardous waste if the incoming trash contains heavy metals, posing long-term disposal risks for the region.
  • Public Health Burdens: By placing these facilities in environmental justice communities like Pittsburg, advocates argue that "hydrogen hubs" risk becoming a new source of industrial pollution rather than a solution for it.
Colusa Indian Energy
Hydrogen Energy & Microgrids  ·  Guidiville Indian Rancheria, California  ·  Tribal Energy Company
The Technology
Colusa Indian Energy (CIE) is the only Tribal-owned microgrid developer in the country, building customized microgrids using a wide range of energy sources and technologies. While their focus is on microgrids, they often "stack" technologies, including solar, geothermal, battery storage (BESS), and biomass, and have incorporated blue and green hydrogen technologies into some of their projects. While they aim for sustainability, they do not claim to be a strictly renewable company, as they also provide energy solutions for Tribes with oil and gas reserves.
Project Overview
A key project is the partnership with the Guidiville Indian Rancheria to install a solar-hydrogen microgrid. Through partnerships with Emtel and Harnyss this project incorporated solar, energy storage, and hydrogen technologies to create a green hydrogen microgrid for the community. The project was originally set to receive funds from federal grants which were later frozen under the Trump administration, causing the Tribe to pivot to loans to complete development. By reducing reliance on the main utility grid, this green hydrogen microgrid project supports Tribal energy sovereignty.
Community Engagement
As a Tribal-owned company, Colusa Indian Energy demonstrates how energy projects can address Tribal energy sovereignty while aiming for a more sustainable future. By prioritizing the training and hiring of Tribal members to build and maintain these systems, CIE ensures that technical expertise and economic benefits remain within the community. Furthermore, CIE acknowledges the diverse economic realities of Tribal Nations by offering pathways for Tribes with oil and gas reserves to transition toward lower-carbon energy. By providing the option for Blue Hydrogen (methane with carbon capture), they allow these Tribes to utilize their existing natural resources while working toward a cleaner energy future. The Guidiville project further serves as a case study in resilience, demonstrating how Tribal leadership can navigate political shifts and "funding freezes" by pivoting financial strategies to keep a project alive.
Key Takeaways
  • Customized Transitions: Their approach allows Tribes to choose between energy technologies that best fits based on their specific land resources and economic needs.
  • Sovereignty through Skills: Their model shows that true independence comes not just from the hardware, but also from developing a Tribal member workforce capable of managing the technology.
  • Baseline vs. Renewable: Because their toolkit includes natural gas and dual-fuel generators, a project may not be 100% "carbon-free." Tribes should clarify if the goal is absolute decarbonization or just grid independence.

The Greenwashing Guide

Buzzwords, Red Flags & What They Really Mean

Greenwashing refers to the practice of marketing a product or project as environmentally friendly when it is not, or when the environmental claims are misleading, incomplete, or unverifiable. Energy developers often use greenwashing language when pitching projects to build trust and fast-track approvals. This section provides a plain-language guide to common terms and what to watch for.

It is worth noting that greenwashing by companies may not always be intentional. Some developers genuinely believe in their technology or are simply repeating industry slogans they haven't fully investigated. However, whether the misleading language is a deliberate tactic or just a result of corporate over-enthusiasm, the result is the same: the community is left with an incomplete picture of the risks. Regardless of a company's intent, the burden of verifying these claims falls on the community.

Buzzword Glossary

Greenwashing Terms Translation
"Zero Emission" / "Zero Emission Facility" A claim that the plant releases nothing into the environment. In reality, no industrial facility is truly "zero" impact. This often ignores emissions from construction, backup generators, or the toxic ash left behind after waste is processed.
"Carbon Neutral" / "Net Zero" The claim that carbon emissions are "canceled out" by planting trees or using carbon capture. Being "neutral" on paper does not mean "pollution-free" for your air; the facility can still release dangerous chemicals into the local community.
"Non-Combustion" / "No-Burn" Technology" The claim that waste is broken down without a flame. While technically different from a traditional fire, the process still uses extreme heat to create gases and toxic residues that must be managed and can leak (Earthjustice Notes).
"Clean Hydrogen" / "Green Hydrogen" No universal legal definition. "Clean hydrogen" under current IRA guidance includes blue hydrogen (fossil fuel-based with CCS), which is not green hydrogen. Projects may claim clean hydrogen status while using natural gas as a primary input.
"Thermal Conversion" / "Advanced Conversion" Fancy names for heating waste to turn it into gas or fuel. These terms are used to hide the fact that the process can release "forever chemicals" (PFAS) and heavy metals into the local environment (Earthjustice Notes).
"Eco-friendly" / "Natural" / "Green" "Fluff" words used to create a positive feeling (Trellis, 2023). These have no legal definition and are often used to distract from the massive amounts of water or chemicals the project actually requires.
"Circular Economy" / "Closed Loop" A system where waste is supposedly reused, promoting recyclability while ignoring the high-emission production of the product itself. In Waste-to-Energy, this is often just a way to say "burning waste," which can release toxic chemicals into the air and leave hazardous residue behind.
"Community Benefit Agreement" A contract promising money or services to the community, and can be meaningful if legally binding and independently negotiated. Frequently used as a goodwill gesture without enforceable obligations. The presence of a CBA does not indicate a project is environmentally sound.

Phrases to Watch For

Developers may use these phrases that make industrial plants sound harmless. If you hear these, you should consider deeper scrutiny of claims.

  • "Environmentally Progressive": Used to frame the facility as a modern, superior alternative to old waste methods (CERREL, 2003). It suggests that traditional waste management is "outdated" to make gasification sound like an inevitable step forward, even if it brings new risks.
  • "Proven To Be Safe": Often used for Carbon Capture (CCS) or Hydrogen pipelines, despite a 50-year track record of high costs, technical failures, and safety risks like pipe embrittlement (Earthjustice Notes; Timalsina et al., 2025).
  • "Waste is Renewable Energy": Just because trash is constantly produced does not make it "renewable" like wind or sun. Processing waste creates toxic byproducts that do not exist with true renewable energy sources.
  • "Alternative to Landfills": This frames the project as the only other option. It ignores better alternatives like true zero-waste programs, composting, and reducing plastic use.
  • "Proprietary Information": This is a legal shield used to hide the specific list of chemicals a company uses or the exact amount of pollution they will release.
  • "Clean Hydrogen Hub": A massive network of plants and pipelines. The "Hub" title creates intense pressure for quick signatures to meet federal funding deadlines, often at the expense of community review (DOE Roadmap).

The Due Diligence Checklist

10 Questions to Ask Before Engaging with Any Energy Developer

Use this checklist before any formal engagement or agreement with an energy developer. These questions are designed to surface greenwashing, protect sovereignty, and ensure your community has the information needed to make a truly informed decision. You have the right to ask all of these questions — and to receive complete, written answers before proceeding.

About the Technology
1
What type of hydrogen or WTE technology is being proposed — exactly — and what is the energy source? Do not accept generic terms like "clean hydrogen," "thermal conversion," or "advanced energy." Ask for the specific process name, the feedstock (what goes in), and the energy source. If it is hydrogen, ask what color and demand documentation of the electricity source.
2
What are the projected air, water, and soil emissions at the project site — not just at the "system" level? Developers often present lifecycle or "system-level" emissions that exclude on-site impacts. Ask for specific emission estimates at the facility location, including all criteria pollutants (NOx, particulates, VOCs), any toxic air contaminants, and wastewater discharge volumes.
3
Has an independent environmental impact assessment been conducted — one not funded by the developer? Developer-funded environmental studies have significant conflicts of interest. Demand an independent review. If one has not been conducted, this should be a prerequisite before any agreement.
About the Company
4
Has this company operated a similar project before — and what was the outcome? Ask for references from completed projects, not just proposals or pilots. Research the company's track record independently. For H-Cycle, research the Plasco Energy Group connection. For any company, search for news coverage, regulatory violations, and community complaints at prior sites.
5
Does the company have a history of regulatory violations, fines, or community complaints? Search EPA's ECHO database (echo.epa.gov) for violations. Search state environmental agency records. Contact environmental justice organizations in areas where the company has operated.
6
Is this company affiliated with — or a rebranding of — a failed project elsewhere? Research the leadership team's prior affiliations. Companies with failed projects frequently rebrand or restructure. H-Cycle's leadership ties to Plasco Energy Group is a documented example of this pattern.
About the Process
7
Were community members—not just council leadership—meaningfully consulted and with enough notice before the project was approved? FPIC requires that the full community, not just elected leadership, have the opportunity to participate in decisions. Ask how community input was solicited, who was included, and how it shaped the project design. A presentation to council is not the same as community consultation.
8
What federal or state funding is the developer accessing, and what conditions come with it? Federal funding through IRA or DOE Hydrogen Hubs comes with specific requirements — ask for documentation. Funding conditions may constrain future community decision-making or create obligations that are not immediately apparent.
9
What legally binding protections exist if the developer fails to meet commitments? Ask for specific, enforceable mechanisms — not just promises. Community Benefit Agreements should be reviewed by independent legal counsel before signing. Ask what happens if the company goes bankrupt, is sold, or fails to meet emission standards.
About Alternatives
10
Has the community been presented with genuinely renewable alternatives — solar, wind, community-owned — at comparable scale? Any developer proposing a hydrogen or WTE project should be able to compare it to solar and wind alternatives. If they cannot or will not make this comparison, that is a red flag. See Section 08 for an overview of alternative options.

The Alternatives

Centering Energy Development Decisions Around Just Transition and Indigenous Sovereignty

It is important to recognize that economic opportunity is a necessary mechanism to ensure that Indigenous communities are not left behind in terms of access to healthy land, water, and food resources; equitable access to education, health, and financial opportunities; and the protections which ensure these rights are secured. While some entities may attempt to exploit Indigenous communities by essentially offering what could be considered 'economic blackmail'—where the only options offered are to agree to the "green" energy projects being proposed or risk missing out on potential economic benefits—we must allow communities to question whether or not these projects are right for them, and make decisions which are truly theirs, and also allow them to consider potential alternative uses for their land and water resources.

Hydrogen and waste-to-energy projects—especially those that depend on the continued use of fossil fuels—are not the only path to energy sovereignty, economic development, or the clean energy transition. Below are recommendations for alternatives that prioritize community health, long-term stability, and the restoration of Indigenous lands.

Energy Sovereignty: Community-Owned Renewables

True energy sovereignty means the Tribe owns the power and the profits, rather than hosting a project for an outside developer.

  • Community-Scale Solar & Wind: Investing in solar microgrids or wind farms that are owned and operated by the Tribe. This keeps energy costs low for Tribal members and ensures the revenue stays within the community.
  • Microgrids for Resilience: Small-scale energy systems that can operate independently of the main power grid, ensuring hospitals and community centers stay powered during outages without the risks of high-pressure hydrogen pipelines.
  • Geothermal Heating: Utilizing the natural heat of the Earth for local buildings, providing a steady, carbon-free energy source that does not involve combustion or toxic ash.

Economic Alternatives: Wealth Without Extraction

Economic growth does not need to come at the cost of the environment. These alternatives could build local wealth while protecting the community's resources.

  • Food Sovereignty & Sustainable Agriculture: Developing greenhouses and restoring traditional agricultural practices. This creates local jobs, secures the food supply, and promotes community health.
  • Eco-Tourism and Cultural Education: Leveraging the beauty and history of the land in a way that is controlled by the Tribe, ensuring visitors respect the environment while contributing to the local economy.
  • Green Manufacturing: Hosting facilities that manufacture components for the clean energy transition—such as solar panels or energy-efficient housing—which provide higher-quality, safer long-term jobs than waste gasification.

Land Rematriation & Healing

For lands decimated by past mistreatment, these alternatives offer restoration rather than more industrial development.

  • Bioremediation: Using native plants and fungi to naturally clean contaminated soil. This restores the land to a state where it can once again support traditional gathering and agriculture.
  • Conservation Easements: Protecting sacred or ecologically sensitive areas from any future industrial development, ensuring the land is preserved for future generations.
  • Natural Carbon Sequestration: Restoring wetlands and old-growth forests. These ecosystems naturally pull carbon from the air more effectively than mechanical "Carbon Capture" and provide vital habitat for wildlife.

The Power of "No"

The most powerful tool a community has is the right to say "No." If a project does not align with the long-term health and values of the Tribe, rejecting it or demanding revisions is not a "lost opportunity"—it is an act of sovereignty that protects the community and land for the next generations. True progress is defined by the community, not the developer.

Recommendations for Decision-Makers

Before moving forward with any industrial energy proposal, Tribal leadership and decision-makers should adopt the following protections to ensure the community is not being exploited:

  • Prioritize Due Diligence: Use a comprehensive due diligence checklist before any formal engagement with a developer. Prioritize meaningful community engagement at the very beginning of the process, not after deals are signed.
  • Ensure Independent Oversight: Require independent legal advice and environmental assessments. Do not rely on studies funded by the developer. Seek out pro bono organizations that specialize in technical and legal reviews for Tribes.
  • Insist on Economic Transparency: Demand a detailed analysis of actual economic potential. Ensure that "jobs" means permanent, high-quality positions with training pathways, supporting a truly Just Transition.
  • Seek Sovereignty-Focused Partners: If the Tribe chooses to pursue these technologies, prioritize partnerships with developers or Tribal energy companies whose primary goal is to secure Tribal Energy Sovereignty rather than corporate profit.
